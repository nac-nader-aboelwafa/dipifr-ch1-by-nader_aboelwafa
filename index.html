IASB & The Regulatory Framework

🇬🇧 English

Globalisation means businesses operate and raise capital across borders. For investors to compare companies from different countries, they need one common accounting language. The IASB creates IFRS Accounting Standards to provide this. Without them, a company in Egypt and one in Germany would report profits completely differently — making investment decisions unreliable.

🇸🇦 شرح بالعربية

العولمة جعلت الشركات تعمل وتجمع رأس المال عبر الحدود. لكي يتمكن المستثمرون من مقارنة الشركات من دول مختلفة، يحتاجون إلى لغة محاسبية موحدة. مجلس معايير المحاسبة الدولي (IASB) يضع معايير IFRS لتحقيق هذا الهدف.

❓ WHY Does IFRS Require This?

Capital markets are global. Without uniform standards, capital markets become inefficient, cost of capital rises, and cross-border investment shrinks. A single standard creates transparency, comparability, and economic efficiency across all jurisdictions.

IFRS Foundation Structure

BodyRoleKey Point
Monitoring BoardPublic accountability of the FoundationCapital markets authorities; formal link to public sector
IFRS Foundation TrusteesGovernance, strategy, oversightAppoint IASB/ISSB members; approve budget
IASBDevelop IFRS Accounting StandardsIndependent; privately funded; 14+ members
ISSBDevelop IFRS Sustainability StandardsFormed 2021; first 2 standards June 2023
IFRS Advisory CouncilAdvises IASB, ISSB & TrusteesPublic meetings; diverse backgrounds
Interpretations CommitteeInterprets existing IFRS StandardsSame authority as IFRS standards

IFRS: Advantages vs Disadvantages

✅ Advantages

  • Compare financial statements across borders
  • Facilitates cross-border stock exchange listing
  • Common accounting language for multinationals
  • Easier appraisal of foreign acquisition targets
  • Lower cost of capital via efficient markets

❌ Disadvantages

  • High implementation costs
  • Less detail vs US GAAP (rules-based)
  • Principles-based = more judgment required
  • US accountants lose litigation defence under GAAP
  • Transition disruption for countries with own standards

🧠 Memory Hack — IFRS Foundation Structure

M · T · B · I
MMonitoring Board → Public accountability (top of pyramid)
TTrustees → Governance, appoint members, approve budget
BBoards (IASB + ISSB) → Develop standards independently
IInterpretations Committee → Clarify & interpret standards
Think: Shareholders (Monitoring) → Board (Trustees) → Management (IASB/ISSB) → Legal (Interpretations)
📌 Key Takeaways
  • IFRS Foundation = not-for-profit, private sector
  • IASB = independent standard-setter
  • Standards do NOT apply to immaterial items
  • Standards are NOT retrospective
🚨 Exam Alert
  • IFRS Foundation has NO enforcement powers — enforcement is national
  • Prior to 2003 = IAS standards; from 2003 = IFRS standards
  • Both IAS and IFRS tested under "IFRS Accounting Standards"

Developing IFRS Standards — The Due Process

🇬🇧 English

IFRS standards follow a formal, transparent, public process so all global stakeholders can contribute. The process moves from advisory advice → discussion paper → exposure draft → final standard. This normally takes years.

🇸🇦 شرح بالعربية

تتبع معايير IFRS عملية رسمية وشفافة وعلنية تضمن مشاركة جميع أصحاب المصلحة حول العالم. تبدأ بمشورة استشارية، ثم ورقة نقاش، ثم مسودة معرضة للتعليق، وأخيراً المعيار النهائي.

The 4-Step Due Process

1

Advisory Committee — IASB establishes an advisory group; consultation with IFRS Advisory Council throughout

2

Discussion Paper — Published for public comment (exploring the issue broadly)

3

Exposure Draft (ED) — A near-final proposed standard for public comment (normally 120 days; minimum 30 days)

4

Final IFRS Accounting Standard — Issued after reviewing all comments received on the ED

❓ WHY This Process?

Three underpinning principles: (a) Transparency — open, public process; (b) Full and fair consultation — all global perspectives considered; (c) Accountability — IASB explains decisions made. This legitimises standards internationally across 140+ countries.

🚨 Exam Trap

Draft Interpretations from the Interpretations Committee are exposed for 90 days — NOT 120 days. Standard comment period = 120 days. Exceptional = 30 days.

📌 Must Remember
  • Scope limitations stated within each individual standard
  • Standards NOT applied to immaterial items
  • IASB is responsible for standard-setting (True — exam Q)

ISSB, IFRS S1 & IFRS S2 — Sustainability Disclosure

🇬🇧 English

The world demands companies disclose not just profits, but how they affect the planet, society, and people. The ISSB (formed 2021) creates global sustainability disclosure standards. IFRS S1 covers all sustainability risks & opportunities. IFRS S2 zooms in on climate. Both effective from 1 January 2024.

🇸🇦 شرح بالعربية

يطالب العالم الشركات بالإفصاح عن تأثيرها على البيئة والمجتمع والناس. معيار IFRS S1 يغطي جميع مخاطر الاستدامة، بينما يركز IFRS S2 على المناخ. كلاهما ساري من 1 يناير 2024.

IFRS S1 — Four Core Content Areas

HeadingPurposeKey Disclosures
🏛 GovernanceWho oversees sustainability risks/opportunitiesWhich body is responsible; management's role in governance
🎯 StrategyHow sustainability affects business model & plansRisks & opportunities expected to affect prospects; effects on business model
⚠️ Risk ManagementHow sustainability risks are identified, assessed & monitoredInputs used; scenario analysis; how risk effects are assessed
📊 Metrics & TargetsProgress toward sustainability targetsRequired ISSB metrics; entity's own metrics; definitions & assumptions

IFRS S2 — Climate Risk Types

🌡 Physical Risks

Acute: Weather events — storms, floods, drought (sudden, event-driven)


Chronic: Long-term climate shifts — sea level rise, biodiversity loss (slow, systemic)


Example: A coastal factory faces flooding risk (acute) and rising sea levels (chronic).

🔄 Transition Risks

Risks arising from moving toward a lower-carbon economy:

  • New carbon taxes or regulations
  • Consumer shift to carbon-neutral alternatives
  • Stranded assets (e.g. oil reserves becoming unusable)
  • Changes in technology or market preferences

🧠 Memory Hack — IFRS S1 Core Content

G · S · R · M
GGovernance — Who's in charge of sustainability?
SStrategy — How does it affect our plans?
RRisk Management — How do we identify & manage risks?
MMetrics & Targets — Are we hitting our goals?
Easy phrase: "Good Strategy Requires Measurement"
🚨 ISSB Key Facts
  • ISSB formed: 2021
  • First 2 standards issued: June 2023
  • Effective from: 1 January 2024
  • Early adoption permitted if both S1 + S2 applied together
📌 Key Definitions

Impacts: Effect the entity has ON stakeholders/environment (GHG emissions, water usage)


Dependencies: Entity's RELIANCE on stakeholders/environment (worker health, resource availability)

Ethical & Professional Principles — The 5 Fundamentals

🔴 CRITICAL EXAM NOTE: Ethics appears in Question 2 of EVERY DipIFR exam for up to 5 marks. Candidates who simply list principles without applying them to the scenario score poorly. Always connect each principle to the specific facts given.

🇬🇧 English

Professional accountants are bound by the IESBA Code of Ethics. This sets out 5 fundamental principles they must uphold. When these principles face threats, the accountant must take steps to eliminate or reduce those threats to an acceptable level.

🇸🇦 شرح بالعربية

يلتزم المحاسبون المهنيون بقواعد الأخلاقيات IESBA. تُحدد هذه القواعد خمسة مبادئ أخلاقية أساسية. عند تهديد هذه المبادئ يجب على المحاسب اتخاذ خطوات للقضاء عليها أو تخفيضها لمستوى مقبول.

The 5 Fundamental Ethical Principles

PrincipleDefinitionExample Violation
1. IntegrityStraightforward and honest in all professional and business relationshipsPreparing financial statements known to contain errors
2. ObjectivityNot compromising judgement due to bias, conflict of interest or undue influenceApproving a transaction that benefits a relative
3. Professional Competence & Due CareMaintain knowledge & skills; act diligently per professional standardsApplying an outdated standard you haven't kept up with
4. ConfidentialityRespect confidentiality of information acquired through professional relationshipsSharing client data with a friend at a competitor
5. Professional BehaviourComply with laws and regulations; avoid conduct that discredits the professionMaking false claims about your qualifications

The 5 Threats to Ethical Compliance

ThreatDefinitionReal-World Example
Self-interestFinancial or other interest inappropriately influences judgementSigning off accounts to protect a profit-related bonus
Self-reviewReviewing your own previous judgement without proper evaluationAuditing financial statements you helped prepare
AdvocacyPromoting a client's position to the point objectivity is compromisedAggressively defending a client's aggressive tax position
FamiliarityToo sympathetic to a client/employer due to close relationshipNot challenging a long-serving client's accounting estimates
IntimidationDeterred from acting objectively by actual or perceived pressureCEO threatening to dismiss the Finance Director if he reports a problem

Addressing Threats — The 3-Step Framework

1

IDENTIFY the threat to compliance with fundamental principles

2

EVALUATE the significance of the threat

3

ADDRESS by: (a) eliminating circumstances; (b) applying safeguards; (c) declining/ending the activity

🧠 Memory Hack — The 5 Principles

I · O · P · C · P
"In Our Profession, Clients Protect"
IIntegrity — Be honest, don't deceive
OObjectivity — No bias or conflicts of interest
PProfessional Competence & Due Care — Stay up to date
CConfidentiality — Don't share client secrets
PProfessional Behaviour — Don't discredit the profession
5 Threats: SSAFI — Smart Students Avoid Failing Integrity
🚨 Examiner Warning

Candidates who list principles with NO link to the scenario get very few marks. You must say: "This creates a self-interest threat to objectivity because [specific fact from scenario]..."

⭐ Exam Scenario Template

Step 1: Name the principle threatened.
Step 2: Name the specific threat type.
Step 3: Explain WHY using scenario facts.
Step 4: Suggest what action to take.

📝 Exam-Style Practice Questions

Test your knowledge with MCQs, short answers, and full scenario questions.

📌 Section A — Multiple Choice Questions

📋 Section B — Short Answer Questions

Short Q 1
Explain two advantages and two disadvantages of adopting IFRS Accounting Standards. (4 marks)
Advantages (any 2):
• Businesses can present financial statements on the same basis as foreign competitors (1 mark)
• Cross-border listing is facilitated, making it easier to raise capital internationally (1 mark)
• Multinationals can use a single company-wide accounting language (1 mark)

Disadvantages (any 2):
• High cost of implementing IFRS standards (1 mark)
• Less prescriptive than US GAAP — requires more professional judgement (1 mark)
• Some accountants may lose litigation protection under detailed national rules (1 mark)
Short Q 2
List the four steps of the IASB's due process for developing a new IFRS Accounting Standard. (4 marks)
Step 1: IASB establishes an Advisory Committee to advise on issues (1 mark)
Step 2: IASB publishes a Discussion Paper for public comment (1 mark)
Step 3: IASB publishes an Exposure Draft — normally 120 days (1 mark)
Step 4: IASB reviews comments and issues the final IFRS Accounting Standard (1 mark)
Short Q 3
State the five fundamental ethical principles under the IESBA Code. (5 marks)
1. Integrity — to be straightforward and honest in all professional and business relationships
2. Objectivity — not to compromise professional judgement because of bias, conflict of interest or undue influence
3. Professional competence and due care — to maintain skills and act diligently
4. Confidentiality — to respect confidentiality of information acquired
5. Professional behaviour — to comply with relevant laws and avoid conduct that discredits the profession

🎯 Section C — Scenario-Based (Exam Style)

Scenario Q 1 — Ethics (5 marks)
Scenario: Nadia is a Finance Manager at Selton Co. Her Finance Director has asked her to prepare a report that significantly overstates the value of certain assets. Nadia knows the valuations are not supportable. The Finance Director has told her that if she refuses, he will give her a poor performance review, affecting her year-end bonus.

Required: Identify and explain the ethical issues Nadia faces and advise what action she should take. (5 marks)
Issue 1 — Integrity (1 mark): Nadia is being asked to prepare a report she knows contains materially misleading information (overstated asset values). This directly threatens her principle of integrity.

Issue 2 — Intimidation Threat (1 mark): The Finance Director's threat to give a poor performance review is an intimidation threat, designed to deter Nadia from acting objectively.

Issue 3 — Self-Interest / Objectivity (1 mark): Her bonus creates a personal financial interest (self-interest threat) that may compromise her objective professional judgement.

Recommended Actions (2 marks):
• Nadia should decline to prepare the report as presented — doing so would breach her ethical obligations
• She should escalate to a more senior level — e.g., the Audit Committee or non-executive directors
• If internal escalation fails, she may need to consider reporting to her professional body
• Nadia should document her concerns and the steps taken to protect herself professionally
Scenario Q 2 — Ethics (5 marks)
Scenario: Omar is an external auditor who has been engaged by Bright Co for 15 years. He has developed a very close friendship with the Finance Director. This year, the Finance Director has asked Omar to provide both the audit and the bookkeeping services. Omar's firm will earn 60% of its total annual fee income from Bright Co.

Required: Identify the ethical threats arising and explain their significance. (5 marks)
Familiarity Threat (1 mark): Omar's 15-year relationship and close friendship with the Finance Director creates a significant familiarity threat to his objectivity. He may be too sympathetic to the Finance Director's views.

Self-Review Threat (1 mark): Providing both bookkeeping and audit services means Omar would be auditing records he himself prepared. This creates a self-review threat — he cannot objectively evaluate his own work.

Self-Interest Threat (1 mark): Earning 60% of total fee income from one client creates a severe self-interest threat. Omar may fear losing this client and compromise his independence to retain the relationship.

Actions required (2 marks):
• Bookkeeping and audit services should NOT be provided simultaneously — one must be declined
• The 60% fee dependency is well above professional guidance thresholds — Omar should either resign from the engagement or significantly reduce the fee dependency
• Consider rotating the audit partner given the length of the engagement
• Document all threats and the firm's responses
